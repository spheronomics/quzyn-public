## The Food Freedom Act Framework

The Institute for Justice's **Model Food Freedom Act** is the policy foundation for Quzyn's platform design.

**Source:** https://ij.org/legislation/food-freedom-act/

**Key provisions:**
- Allows unrestricted sale of "not potentially hazardous foods" (shelf-stable, no refrigeration needed — baked goods, jams, etc.) when properly labeled as homemade
- Allows sale of "potentially hazardous homemade foods" with direct-to-consumer delivery requirements
- Positions home-based food entrepreneurship as an economic opportunity especially for stay-at-home parents, the disabled, farmers, military spouses, and immigrants

**Quzyn's positioning:** Quzyn is the platform infrastructure that makes the Food Freedom Act work at scale — providing trust, verification, logistics, and branded packaging so home cooks can operate as real businesses.

## State-by-State Reference

### 🤠 Texas
**Status:** Very Favorable
**Official source:** https://www.dshs.texas.gov/retail-food-establishments/texas-cottage-food-production
**Guide:** https://texascottagefoodlaw.com

| Requirement | Status |
|---|---|
| Food Handler Certificate | Not required by state — **required by Quzyn** |
| Kitchen inspection | ❌ Not required |
| Food establishment license | ❌ Not required for qualifying cottage food |
| Gross sales cap | ✅ Removed (post-2023 reform) |
| Labeling | ✅ Required (name, address, ingredients, allergens, "made in home kitchen") |
| Sales channels | Direct-to-consumer, internet, phone orders allowed |
| Delivery | Allowed |

**Quzyn priority state.** Start here. No barriers to entry.
